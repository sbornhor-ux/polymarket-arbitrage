## Booth Team Launches Digital Fama: A Real-Time Platform That Translates Prediction Market Signals Into Financial Instrument Alerts

**CHICAGO, March 2026** — A five-person team of MBA students at the University of Chicago Booth School of Business has built and deployed Digital Fama, a real-time prediction-market intelligence platform that monitors more than 25,000 active Polymarket contracts, identifies probability swings relevant to finance and macroeconomics, and maps those swings to correlated traditional financial instruments. The system surfaces its findings on a public live dashboard, runs continuously on cloud infrastructure costing approximately $5 per month, and generates daily structured investment reports.

The project addresses a specific blind spot in financial data workflows. Prediction markets aggregate crowd beliefs through real-money bets and produce prices bounded in the range [0, 1], often reflecting new information about policy outcomes, geopolitical events, and macroeconomic data before that information is fully priced into traditional financial assets. No systematic, automated tool previously existed to detect those probability shifts, map them to affected financial instruments, and test whether a statistically meaningful divergence between the prediction market's implied move and the financial market's actual move is present. Digital Fama provides exactly that.

The system operates as a five-stage pipeline through four named components. The **Polymarket Data Interpreter** ingests real-time 1-minute probability ticks from Polymarket's CLOB API, resamples them to 15-minute intervals using the last observed probability (preserving discrete state semantics), and applies a deterministic relevance scoring system before passing data downstream: Federal Reserve and monetary-policy markets receive a relevance score of 0.90, earnings markets score 0.82, mergers and acquisitions score 0.78, while sports and crypto markets are penalized and filtered out. Of the resulting finance-relevant markets, each receives a composite score weighting market volume (40%), odds-swing magnitude (35%), and volume surge (25%); those in the top half by composite score and above a minimum score threshold proceed to deep analysis. Swing detection runs a rolling z-score against a 30-day trailing baseline across four time windows — 1-hour, 6-hour, 1-day, and 5-day — and declares a swing when the z-score exceeds 2.0, confirming that a given probability move is statistically unusual for that specific market. The **Polymarket-to-Financial Matcher** then runs a two-call LLM pipeline for each confirmed swing: a first call brainstorms seven US-listed candidate instruments across individual stocks, sector ETFs, and macro ETFs; a second call critically evaluates those seven, selects the 2–3 with the strongest and most direct exposure, and writes a structured three-sentence rationale for each, along with a directional alignment tag ("with" or "against") and a calibrated 1-to-100 confidence score. All tickers are validated against Polygon.io before use, with smart fallbacks from index tickers to ETF equivalents and commodities routed to USO and GLD. The **Trend Analyst** then runs six statistical tests — Pearson/Spearman correlation, Granger causality, lead-lag cross-correlation (±10 hours), event study, spike event study, and volatility spillover — with weights dynamically assigned by an LLM based on the market question, sector, instrument type, and data availability, using absolute ΔP rather than log-returns throughout (because prediction-market prices are bounded at [0, 1] with flat periods and binary terminal jumps that make log-returns ill-defined). The **Synthesiser** converts these results into natural-language investment reports.

The core signal generated by Digital Fama is a **divergence signal**: the Trend Analyst fits a historical beta (β) from the prediction-market probability series to the financial instrument's price series, and then compares the financial market's actual move during a live swing against what the beta predicts it should have moved. When the financial market has moved significantly less than the model predicts, Digital Fama flags an underreaction — a condition where crowd beliefs have shifted but prices in the traditional market have not yet caught up. This is the information asymmetry that event-driven investors can act on. The divergence signal is the product's primary output and the mechanism by which it transforms raw prediction-market data into an actionable financial signal.

The system is live today. A public dashboard at https://sbornhor-ux.github.io/polymarket-arbitrage/ displays current market metadata, composite scores, per-market summaries, instrument rationale with confidence scores, and statistical test results for each flagged market. The pipeline runs on Railway with a configurable scan interval, using Cloudflare R2 for persistent storage (free tier: 10 GB, 10 million reads per month) and GitHub Pages for the dashboard front-end. Results are also available in JSON and CSV via public R2 URLs for programmatic consumption. Total infrastructure cost is approximately $5 per month. Daily summary reports are generated at 4:00 PM CST. The pipeline uses Pydantic data contracts throughout and implements exponential backoff on all external API calls.

The current system is a signal-detection and monitoring tool, not a trading system. It does not connect to a brokerage, execute orders, or provide backtested performance. The divergence signal establishes that the financial market has moved less than historical beta predicts; it does not guarantee the gap will close in the expected direction or on a predictable timeline. Coverage is currently limited to Polymarket; other prediction venues — Kalshi, Manifold Markets — are not yet integrated. The pipeline requires API keys for Polygon.io, OpenAI, and Cloudflare R2. Planned extensions include a backtesting loop to validate identified picks against historical data, more advanced filtering for unusual market movements and potential insider-information signals, expanded venue coverage, and an exploration of the same divergence-signal framework applied to sports-betting markets, where Polymarket odds frequently diverge from major sportsbooks before line adjustments.

# Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Who is the target customer?**

The primary customer is an event-driven or macro-oriented investor — a discretionary hedge fund analyst, a systematic strategy desk, or a family office portfolio manager — whose investment thesis depends on anticipating how political, regulatory, or macroeconomic outcomes will affect specific financial instruments. These investors already monitor news flow and economic data; Digital Fama adds a real-time, quantified view of crowd beliefs with instrument-level mappings and a divergence signal they would otherwise have to construct manually. A secondary customer is any researcher or practitioner building alternative-data workflows who wants a structured, programmatic feed of prediction-market signals mapped to financial assets.

**2. What problem does this solve?**

Prediction markets aggregate dispersed information efficiently, often before that information reaches mainstream financial data feeds. But translating a prediction-market probability shift into a tradeable view requires three steps that practitioners currently do manually: identifying which financial instruments are affected, establishing whether a statistical relationship between the prediction-market series and the instrument series exists, and determining whether the financial market has actually responded to the shift or whether an exploitable underreaction is still present. Digital Fama automates all three steps and presents the output — including a divergence signal flagging the gap between expected and actual financial market movement — in a structured, daily-updated format.

**3. How does the multi-stage pipeline work?**

The pipeline has five stages. First, the Polymarket CLOB API is polled continuously, ingesting 1-minute probability ticks for all active contracts (25,000+ at time of writing). Second, the Polymarket Data Interpreter resamples each series to 15-minute intervals, applies deterministic relevance scores (Fed/monetary: 0.90, earnings: 0.82, M&A: 0.78; sports and crypto are penalized), computes composite scores weighting market volume (40%), odds-swing magnitude (35%), and volume surge (25%), and detects swings via rolling z-score (z ≥ 2.0 versus a 30-day trailing baseline) across four windows (1H, 6H, 1D, 5D). Third, the Polymarket-to-Financial Matcher runs a two-call LLM pipeline to select the 2–3 traditional instruments with the strongest exposure, each validated against Polygon.io, with directional alignment and calibrated confidence scores. Fourth, the Trend Analyst runs six statistical tests — Pearson/Spearman correlation, Granger causality, lead-lag cross-correlation (±10 hours), event study, spike event study, and volatility spillover — with weights dynamically assigned by an LLM; it also computes the divergence signal by fitting historical β and flagging financial market underreaction. Fifth, the Synthesiser converts results into natural-language investment reports. Financial data is fetched at adaptive granularity (15-minute bars for recent data, hourly up to 2 years, daily beyond that) with ±24-hour off-hours expansion.

**4. Why not just trade Polymarket directly?**

Polymarket is a binary-outcome market with limited liquidity in most contracts, US-resident access restrictions for real-money trading, and resolution timelines that can span months. A trader who believes the crowd is mispricing a political outcome on Polymarket may not be able to put on a meaningful position there. The more liquid expression of the same view is through a correlated traditional instrument — an ETF, a Treasury bond, a commodity future — that trades continuously, has deep order books, and does not require a binary outcome resolution to generate returns if the view is correct. Digital Fama finds those liquid expressions automatically and quantifies how much the traditional market has already moved relative to what historical beta predicts.

**5. How does the LLM instrument selection work?**

When a statistically significant swing is detected, the system calls an LLM twice. The first call prompts the model to brainstorm seven candidate financial instruments broadly, following explicit coverage rules: always include a sector ETF relevant to the theme; include the specific company if one is named in the question; include macro ETFs (TLT, GLD, USO) for policy questions; default to bank stocks (JPM, GS, BAC) for Fed questions, and defense names (LMT, NOC, RTX) for geopolitical questions. The second call critically evaluates those seven candidates against the specific market question, selects the 2–3 with the strongest and most direct exposure, and writes a three-sentence rationale for each: the company's business model, the specific mechanism by which this market outcome affects it, and an honest strength assessment with caveats. Each selected ticker is then validated against Polygon.io. The confidence score uses the full 1-to-100 range: 90+ means the company is named directly in the question; below 30 indicates tenuous second-order exposure.

**6. What does the live dashboard show?**

The public dashboard at https://sbornhor-ux.github.io/polymarket-arbitrage/ displays the output of the most recent pipeline run. For each flagged market it shows: the market question and metadata (category, end date, current probability, volume, liquidity), the composite score and flags triggered, the selected financial instruments with tickers, alignment direction, confidence scores, and LLM-generated rationales, and the results of the six statistical tests including the divergence signal. Results are also available in JSON and CSV via Cloudflare R2 public URLs for programmatic access. Daily summary reports are generated at 4:00 PM CST.

**7. What are the current limitations?**

Digital Fama is a monitoring and signal-detection tool only — it does not connect to any brokerage or execute trades. No live backtesting engine exists; the divergence signal identifies gaps between predicted and actual financial market movement but does not estimate the probability or magnitude of gap closure. Signal accuracy under live conditions has not been systematically measured, and practitioners should treat the output as one input among several. Coverage is currently Polymarket-only; Kalshi, Manifold Markets, and other venues are not yet integrated. The pipeline requires three API credentials (Polygon.io, OpenAI, Cloudflare R2), and the depth of historical price data available for statistical tests depends on the Polygon.io subscription tier.

**8. How is this different from traditional market-monitoring tools?**

Most market-monitoring tools track price, volume, or news flow in traditional financial markets. Sentiment tools parse text — headlines, earnings transcripts, social media — and produce qualitative signals. Digital Fama uses a different primary input: the real-money probability of a discrete event, as aggregated by a crowd of bettors, bounded in [0, 1] and continuously updated as information arrives. This probability series is then statistically linked to specific financial instruments using historically fitted beta, and the divergence between predicted and realized financial market movement is computed and flagged directly. The result is a structured, quantified underreaction signal that a practitioner can compare to existing market pricing without interpreting raw text, building custom mappings, or conducting the statistical analysis manually.
